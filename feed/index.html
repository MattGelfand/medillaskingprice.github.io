Everyone has to eat. But in the piece of legislation that’s brought an unlikely pair, growers and consumers, to a national stage, someone is bound to lose.

The Man in Manhattan

Jim Robbins, 56, sits alone in the cab of his red Case combine, which is silent save for the occasional garbled chatter over a walkie-talkie. He farms nearly 3,000 acres, the equivalent of more than 2,250 football fields of corn and soybeans.

The weatherman has assured a high of 50 today, typical for early October in Manhattan, Ill., but Robbins’s team of four thickly flannelled farm employees with coffee mugs the size of theme park soda pops know otherwise. Today, the fog will not melt away. The fields will remain wet from yesterday’s rain. It’s cold.

Robbins, though, is warm inside the cab. Dressed to the occasion in a pair of mud-caked work boots and a gray shirt that covers his round belly, he seems comfortable with the task of tackling this year’s harvest. And he has the U.S. government as a necessary backup just in case of harsh weather and falling crop prices, he says. Last year, he received $146,000 in federally supported aid – $46,000 in direct payments and $100,000 for crop insurance.

The subsidies are a product of an 80-year-old Farm Bill that affords Robbins new farm implements, fertilizer and other production inputs such as the combine he sits in now. The legislation, traditionally renewed every 5 years, deals with most things agriculture in the U.S., from green environmental efforts to the annual federal aid Robbins receives. The combine was a quarter-million-dollar investment, but Robbins was able to pay for it entirely in cash thanks to this government aid and five or six years of consistent profit, he says. This year his aid amount is not much different.

If the payments he receives seem like good business for him, it’s because they are. But, he says, they’re good business for the economy, too. “When a farmer gets some money,” Robbins says, “It gets turned back into the system pretty quick.”

“Overall, I mean, I think, we got the best system in the world.”

Millions of golden corn kernels ping pong around in the tank behind him. The larger the investment in farming, the more society benefits, he says, his voice doing little to hide his rural Midwestern roots. “If you like to eat, we contribute. If we don’t raise anything, people don’t have anything to eat.”

"Well if you like to eat, we contribute."

There’s a real lack of understanding about what it takes to get all those corn and soy-based products into supermarkets, if you ask him. “A lot of people, they think where they go to the grocery store, that’s where the food comes from,” he says. “That’s not where the food comes from … It originates out in the field, and somebody raised that to make that product at the store.”

To most, Robbins seems like a typical recipient of Farm Bill funding. His occupation matches the bill’s title after all. What might not be as apparent, though, is that the funding farmers like him receive accounts for just 23 percent of the bill.

The Farm Bill isn’t all about farms. A legislative battle is brewing over the other 77 percent: nutrition programs. Last summer, though, the House of Representatives proposed and passed a $39 billion cut to the largest chunk of nutrition funding, the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program, more commonly known as food stamps. It’s a decision that is radically threatening a long-standing and unlikely union. For decades, the bill has connected farmers and SNAP recipients by one common thread: political survival.

An Unlikely Marriage

The Farm Bill grew out of an era of economic collapse. The U.S. was in the thick of the Great Depression after World War I, and President Franklin D. Roosevelt proposed the New Deal, a series of economic programs aimed at boosting and stabilizing a ruined economy. The Agricultural Adjustment Act, which would later become the Farm Bill, was one of the hallmark New Deal programs when it passed in 1933. The bill provided farmers income supports, giving a then much-larger rural population better access to food.

“The rural economy was seen as an agricultural economy at that point,” says Anne Effland, a farm policy specialist with the United States Department of Agriculture. “If the farm economy is healthy, then the rural economy will be healthy, and the national economy will be healthy.”

The nutrition portion, though, which now accounts for $209 billion of the Farm Bill, wasn’t always a part of the legislation. Nutrition programs have existed since the New Deal, but rapid inflation and increased urbanization led more Americans to turn to federal nutrition assistance in the 1970s.

At the same time, a shrinking rural population lacked enough political power to pass agricultural programs, according to Effland. Not wanting their farming constituents’ interests to go unserved, politicians from agricultural districts sought out urban political allies as a kind of safety net, and a new Farm Bill was born.

Both the nutrition and agriculture policy makers needed more votes to maintain funding for their respective programs. “It was added out of survival,” Harrison Pittman, director of the National Agricultural Law Center, says. “The food stamp program realized it was under attack, and they realized there was going to be a Farm Bill [up for renewal]. So the Food Stamp lobbyists and the Farm Bill lobbyists cut a deal.”

Democrats and Republicans thought that by combining the two sides it would be a win-win situation, Pittman says. After the merger, agriculture policy had enough votes to pass their subsidies and nutrition programs had the possibility for more funding.

It was nothing more than a marriage of political convenience.

Today the Farm Bill still funds government policies related to agriculture and the entirety of the SNAP program. The most recent iteration of the bill, called the Food, Conservation and Energy Act of 2008, upheld some of the same agricultural safety-net policies as its predecessors. The government seemed to pass the Farm Bill almost automatically since its inception, but now, for the first time, the Farm Bill is at a standstill, its future unclear.

“Historically, [the Farm Bill has] been pretty much a bipartisan affair and not that controversial, and they've been able to get through the congress pretty well,” Pittman says. “They've gotten more and more difficult to pass, but pretty much, you can make an easy argument that this one is the most contentious.”

In the most recent Farm Bill, funding for agricultural policy composes just $60 billion of the $307 billion budget, according to the Congressional Budget Office. Farmers make up less than 1 percent of the U.S. population, compared with 21 percent in the 1930s, according to the Environmental Protection Agency's Census of Agriculture. But the number of SNAP recipients continues to grow. Currently 15 percent, or 47.6 million, of the U.S. population uses federal nutrition assistance -- more than ever before. This means an unprecedented number of SNAP recipients, who typically vote Democratic, have their monthly food budget tied to a much smaller, largely Republican base.
